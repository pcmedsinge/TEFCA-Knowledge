TEFCA + FHIR + Clinical AI — what's actually happening, and what's actually changing.

You're a healthcare IT practitioner or clinician. You know what interoperability failure looks like from the inside — the duplicate CT, the conflicting med list, the patient who can't be matched across organizations.

This is a deep-dive on what TEFCA is in 2026 (mostly C-CDA over IHE) and what it's becoming (Facilitated FHIR via UDAP). No marketing language. Every claim sourced.

01 · The Problem Wall

Five things every healthcare IT practitioner has seen

Each scenario below is grounded in a peer-reviewed or institutional source. The point isn't to argue these problems exist — you've seen them. The point is to size them.

01

The repeat ED scan

A 54-year-old presents to Hospital A with abdominal pain. She had an abdominal CT three weeks ago at unaffiliated Hospital B across town. Hospital A's ED has no view into Hospital B's PACS or report. She gets another CT. The first one is sitting in a record twelve miles away.

14.7%
of ED CT scans are repeats — same study, same body region, within 30 days at unaffiliated EDs (CA + FL, 2007–2010)
HIE participation cut that by 59% for CT, 44% for ultrasound, 67% for chest x-ray.

Lammers EJ, Adler-Milstein J, Kocher KE. Medical Care. 2014;52(3):227-34.

02

The discharge med list nobody trusts

A 72-year-old is discharged after a CHF admission on nine medications. Her PCP's chart shows seven different ones. Her daughter brings in a third list from the assisted-living facility. Three caregivers, three lists, no source of truth. Everyone makes their best guess.

54%
of admitted patients have at least one potentially harmful medication discrepancy during their stay
75% of those discrepancies happen at discharge. 72% are rooted in errors taking the medication history — not in the reconciliation step itself.

Pippins JR, Gandhi TK, Hamann C, et al. Journal of General Internal Medicine. 2008;23(9):1414-1422.

03

The patient who can't be matched

A patient with a common name moves states and presents to a new health system. Their prior records exist within the destination QHIN's reach, but the demographic match isn't confident enough to return them. The new provider redoes the workup from scratch.

50%
match rates between organizations can drop this low — even between organizations using the same EHR vendor
Within a single facility, duplicate-record rates average 18%. 1 in 5 hospital CIOs report patient harm from mismatches in the prior year.

Pew Charitable Trusts. Enhanced Patient Matching Is Critical to Achieving Full Promise of Digital Health Records. October 2018.

04

The chronically ill patient seeing four specialists

A diabetic patient sees endocrinology, cardiology, nephrology, and primary care. Labs are duplicated quarterly. The cardiologist's diuretic adjustment doesn't make it back to the endocrinologist's chart. A preventable ED visit happens. Each provider holds half the picture.

$4,542
higher annual healthcare spending per chronically ill patient with highly fragmented PCP-referral patterns vs least-fragmented (US commercial claims, n=506,376)
Highly fragmented care: 32.8% vs 25.9% departures from clinical best practice and 9.1% vs 7.1% preventable hospitalization rate.

Frandsen BR, Joynt KE, Rebitzer JB, Jha AK. American Journal of Managed Care. 2015;21(5):355-62.

05

The C-CDA that arrived without the latest update

A specialist receives a C-CDA summary for a referred patient. The document is structurally valid. It just doesn't include the problem-list additions from last month's hospitalization across town — those exist in the originating EHR but never made it into the exchanged document. The first 20 minutes of the visit are reconstruction work.

2026
— and TEFCA exchange is still primarily document-based (C-CDA over IHE XCA/XCPD), not native FHIR
Facilitated FHIR SOP v2.0 (draft, March 2026) is the migration path; UDAP support extends through 2027. Until critical mass arrives, dual-pipeline reality and lossy round-trips between document and structured data.

2026 ASTP Annual Meeting recap; Facilitated FHIR Implementation SOP v2.0 (draft).

02 · The TEFCA Ecosystem

Eleven QHINs, one Common Agreement, and the layer cake underneath

TEFCA isn't one thing. It's a layered governance and exchange model: ONC sets the rules, the RCE operates the network, governing documents define the contracts, eleven Designated Qualified Health Information Networks (QHINs) serve as the hubs, and tiered Participants reach down to the actual endpoints — clinicians, patients, public health, and government benefits programs.

The governing documents

The trio every QHIN, Participant, and Subparticipant signs onto.

v2.1 · Nov 2024

Common Agreement

The master contract. Every QHIN, Participant, and Subparticipant signs onto its current version. v2.0 was published in the Federal Register May 2024; v2.1 superseded it in November 2024.

v2.1 · current

The technical specification QHINs implement. Defines the exchange protocols, security requirements, and interoperability profiles. Cross-referenced in the January 2026 TEFCA Glossary.

Source: TEFCA Glossary (Jan 2026)

v1.0

Participant / Subparticipant Terms of Participation

Standardized terms for Tier 2 (Participant) and Tier 3 (Subparticipant) onboarding. Signed between the QHIN and downstream organizations.

Source: TEFCA Glossary (Jan 2026)

The six Exchange Purposes

Every TEFCA query happens for one of these reasons. Government Benefits Determination is the newest — added in 2025 to support SSA disability determinations.

Treatment

The most common purpose — providers exchanging data for direct patient care. 45 CFR § 164.501 definition.

XP Implementation SOP: Treatment v1.2 (1/16/2026)

Payment

Payers and providers exchanging data for billing, eligibility, and claims processing.

Health Care Operations

Quality measurement, care coordination, credentialing, audits — the operational backbone of health care delivery.

Public Health

Mandated and voluntary reporting to public health authorities — surveillance, registries, disease tracking.

Individual Access Services (IAS)

Helping individuals access their own records — patient-facing apps, portable health records.

IAS Provider Requirements SOP v2.1 (3/17/2026)

NEW

Government Benefits Determination

Supports SSA disability determinations (SSDI Title II, SSI Title XVI). The goal: replace weeks-or-months of record retrieval with minutes-or-seconds.

XP Implementation SOP: Government Benefits Determination v1.0 (released 2025)

03 · Query Flows

Two flows, side by side: today's documents, tomorrow's FHIR

Most TEFCA writeups assume FHIR. Production reality in 2026 is still C-CDA. Here are both flows side by side, and the gap between them — written with enough detail that a TEFCA implementer would not find errors, but simplified to expose the architecture rather than every spec-by-spec wire detail.

Flow A — Document-based 2026 reality

XCPD → XCA → C-CDA XML payload

  1. 1

    Initiator queries

    Clinician at Hospital A queries via the EHR. The query routes through Hospital A's QHIN.

  2. 2

    XCPD patient discovery

    Initiator's QHIN broadcasts XCPD (Cross-Community Patient Discovery) to all other QHINs: "do you know patient X?" Each QHIN forwards to its Participants based on demographic match logic.

  3. 3

    Patient identifiers returned

    Matching Participants respond with patient identifiers and HomeCommunityIDs. Initiator's QHIN aggregates and correlates the patient identities across communities.

  4. 4

    XCA document query

    Initiator's QHIN sends XCA Query (Cross-Community Access) to each community that returned a match, asking for documents that satisfy the requested Exchange Purpose.

  5. 5

    C-CDA documents retrieved

    XCA Retrieve fetches documents (typically C-CDA XML) from each Responding Participant.

  6. 6

    Documents returned to clinician

    Bundle of C-CDA documents lands at the Initiator. The EHR may render them as PDFs, or — if it supports C-CDA section extraction — pull structured snippets into the chart.

Flow B — Facilitated FHIR emerging via SOP v2.0

RCE Directory → UDAP SSRAA → FHIR R4 / US Core v3.1.1

  1. 1

    Initiator queries

    Same starting point as Flow A — a clinician (or a patient app, in IAS) needs patient X's records.

  2. 2

    RCE Directory lookup

    Initiator queries the RCE Directory Service for the Responding Node's FHIR endpoints. IAS variant: requesting app may filter to IAS-enabled endpoints only.

  3. 3

    UDAP SSRAA registration

    Initiator presents a TEFCA-issued certificate and registers as a UDAP Trusted Client per the HL7 UDAP Security for Scalable Registration, Authentication, and Authorization Implementation Guide. Receives a client_id. One-time per Responding Node.

  4. 4

    Authentication + access token

    Initiator authenticates against the Responding Node's authorization server and receives an OAuth 2.0 access token scoped to the requested Exchange Purpose.

  5. 5

    FHIR query for Patient

    GET [base]/Patient?identifier=...&family=...&given=... with the access token. Returns the matched Patient resource (or no match).

  6. 6

    FHIR queries for clinical resources

    Subsequent calls retrieve MedicationRequest, AllergyIntolerance, Condition, Observation, ImagingStudy, DiagnosticReport, Encounter — packaged as FHIR Bundles. IAS variant: includes Consent via consent_reference. GBD variant: scope narrowed to SSA-determined records per the GBD Implementation SOP.

When things go wrong: latency and silent failures

The hard parts aren't in the happy path. These are the realistic failure modes and ballpark timings practitioners actually hit.

A

Document-flow latency, end-to-end

XCPD broadcast plus sequential XCA query/retrieve roundtrips. Each unaffiliated QHIN adds latency; aggregation across communities adds variance.

~30–60s
typical end-to-end for a document-flow query touching 3–4 QHINs (implementer ballpark — re-verify against published benchmarks before publication)
Worst case at the clinical decision point: clinician moves on without the data; scan/lab gets repeated.
B

FHIR-flow per-call vs end-to-end

Each FHIR call is fast — but a full clinical picture means multiple calls. Net assembly time can be comparable to the document flow despite the faster wire protocol.

<1–3s
per FHIR call (implementer ballpark) × 5–10 calls to assemble meds + allergies + problems + imaging + reports
"FHIR is faster" is true only at the per-call level. End-to-end picture-assembly dominates.
C

No patient match returned

XCPD returns nothing for a patient who actually has records elsewhere. Demographic match isn't confident enough across QHINs. Query stops; clinician redoes the workup.

50%
cross-org patient match rate can drop this low — even between organizations using the same EHR vendor
Same failure mode in Flow A and Flow B: the query never reaches the data.

Pew Charitable Trusts. Enhanced Patient Matching Is Critical to Achieving Full Promise of Digital Health Records. October 2018.

D

Consent-filtered response, no alert

Responding QHIN withholds records based on consent (sensitive segments, behavioral health, IAS-restricted scope). The Initiator gets a partial response that looks complete. No standardized error surfaces.

0 alerts
error responses typically returned to the Initiator when a Responding QHIN filters records on consent grounds
Clinician may make decisions on incomplete data without knowing data was withheld. The hardest failure mode to design around.

The honest annotation

Most TEFCA exchange in 2026 is still Flow A. Flow B (Facilitated FHIR) is real and growing, but the SOP v2.0 is still in draft as of March 2026 and UDAP-based registration is permitted only through 2027. Practitioners building today should expect dual-pipeline complexity for at least the next 18–24 months — and design for it explicitly rather than waiting for a clean cutover.

Sources: 2026 ASTP Annual Meeting recap; Facilitated FHIR Implementation SOP v2.0 (draft).

04 · The Clinical AI Unlock

What TEFCA actually unlocks for Clinical AI — and what it doesn't

TEFCA delivers data. Clinical AI consumes data. The pipe matters, but a working pipe doesn't make the model right. This section is about both halves of that sentence — what becomes possible with TEFCA-mediated data ingestion, and where the model still has to earn its keep.

The pipeline (RAG over TEFCA data)

A typical retrieval-augmented generation pipeline grounded in TEFCA-delivered data. Each step is a real engineering decision, not a slide. If you're a clinician, you can skim or skip the pipeline — the four use cases below are the practical takeaway.

TEFCA query

XCPD/XCA (document) or Facilitated FHIR (structured) per Phase 3 flows.

Ingest

C-CDA XML parsing or FHIR Bundle handling. Many teams normalize to FHIR resources at this step (others target OMOP or a proprietary clinical schema).

→
3

Normalize coding

Map to canonical code systems per USCDI v3 data classes (RxNorm, SNOMED CT, LOINC, ICD-10-CM); resolve unit and observation-convention variations; handle missing-data conventions.

→
4

Vector store

Chunk and embed (clinical-domain embedding model). Patient-scoped indices are typical; shared indices with patient-aware retrieval filters are also used.

→
5

Retrieval

Hybrid (semantic + keyword) typically. Filter by date, encounter, source quality. Re-rank for clinical relevance.

→
6

LLM grounding

Retrieved chunks injected into prompt context. Output can cite specific source documents — a deliberate design choice, not automatic. RAG measurably improves accuracy on medical QA tasks.

Source: Amugongo LM, Mascheroni P, Brooks S, Doering S, Seidel J. PLOS Digital Health. 2025;4(6):e0000877 — systematic review of 70 clinical-RAG studies; GPT-4 medical-QA accuracy improved from ~73% to ~80% with RAG.

Four concrete use cases

Where TEFCA-grounded AI lands today, ordered from most-mature to most-aspirational.

Most mature

Care transitions / medication reconciliation

The clinician's true challenge isn't the reconciliation step — it's the history-taking step (Pippins 2008: 72% of harmful medication discrepancies originate there). TEFCA can pull a patient's medication list from QHIN-connected sources where the patient has a record, giving the AI a more complete pre-admission picture than any single EHR holds.

High-volume

Prior authorization automation

PA requires assembling clinical justification from records that often live across multiple care settings. TEFCA + RAG can draft a PA packet by pulling the relevant labs, imaging, and prior treatment failures and structuring them against the payer's criteria.

The honest part: payer-specific form structures change quarterly, criteria are inconsistent across plans, and denials still trigger peer-to-peer review by a clinician. The AI can shorten the first-pass authoring; it doesn't shorten the appeals fight.

Diagnostic context

Longitudinal context for diagnostic AI

Many imaging and pathology AI models benefit from prior studies, prior reports, and trend data — particularly for nodule surveillance, oncology response, and chronic-disease imaging. Magnitude varies by model and modality.

Today, getting outside imaging usually means CD/DVD courier or third-party image-sharing networks (LifeImage, Ambra, Nuance PowerShare). TEFCA + DocumentReference + ImagingStudy resources offer a more streamlined path — once Facilitated FHIR adoption catches up to the document-side reality.

Research / QI

Longitudinal cohort queries

QI teams need patient cohorts assembled across organizational boundaries. TEFCA's Health Care Operations and Public Health Exchange Purposes can support some of this work — though consent-at-scale and de-identification add real friction (see limitations below).

For true research-grade cohort assembly, established networks like PCORnet, OneFlorida+, and ENACT remain the more common route in 2026 — TEFCA isn't designed primarily as a research backbone, and "Research" isn't one of the six Exchange Purposes.

What can go wrong: the Epic Sepsis lesson

The Epic Sepsis Model is a proprietary AI built on rich longitudinal EHR data — exactly the kind of data TEFCA can deliver. At the time of study it was deployed at hundreds of US hospitals.

An external validation at Michigan Medicine (Wong et al., JAMA Internal Medicine 2021; n=27,697 adult patients, 38,455 hospitalizations) found the model performed substantially worse than the developer reported:

  • AUC 0.63 (95% CI: 0.62–0.64) vs Epic's claimed 0.76–0.83
  • Sensitivity 33%, specificity 83%, PPV 12%, NPV 95%
  • Missed 67% of sepsis cases (1,709 of 2,552) despite generating alerts
  • Alerts on 18% of all hospitalizations — substantial alert-fatigue burden

The takeaway for TEFCA + AI: data delivery is necessary but not sufficient. Local validation, calibration, and ongoing monitoring stay the implementer's responsibility. TEFCA solves data delivery; it does not solve model validity.

Source: Wong A, Otles E, Donnelly JP, et al. "External Validation of a Widely Implemented Proprietary Sepsis Prediction Model in Hospitalized Patients." JAMA Internal Medicine. 2021;181(8):1065-1070.

What TEFCA does NOT solve for AI

Honest list. Each of these is the implementer's problem, not TEFCA's.

  • Data quality

    C-CDA documents vary substantially in completeness and structure. USCDI v3 conformance is required from January 2026 forward, but legacy data, incomplete sections, and free-text dumps remain common.

  • Semantic normalization

    Different sources use different code systems (NDC vs RxNorm vs free-text), different units (mg/dL vs mmol/L), different observation conventions. TEFCA delivers; you normalize.

  • Consent-at-scale for AI training and inference

    Treatment-purpose data flows fine. Using TEFCA-delivered data to train AI models, or to run inference on de-identified cohorts, runs into HIPAA research provisions, state-level consent laws, and the §164.514 de-identification standard — a thicket the framework does not yet cleanly resolve.

  • Patient matching is still imperfect

    Pew 2018 documented cross-org match rates as low as 50%. Garbage in (wrong patient) means garbage out (wrong AI conclusion). The same gating problem that limits Flow A and Flow B in Phase 3 limits AI too.

  • Document vs FHIR dual-pipeline burden

    Most TEFCA exchange in 2026 is C-CDA. Most clinical AI tooling assumes FHIR resources. Bridging the two means building C-CDA → FHIR mapping infrastructure that has its own loss profile. Until Facilitated FHIR reaches critical mass, dual-pipeline complexity is the implementer's problem.

05 · The Road Ahead

Where TEFCA goes next, where it doesn't, and what to watch

The honest exit from a knowledge hub: what's still broken, what's actively changing, and where to keep watching.

The honest gaps

Things that exist by design or by 2026 reality, not bugs. Each is its own implementer's problem.

Patient matching

The Phase 1 Pew stat (50% cross-org match rates even with the same EHR vendor) isn't a bug TEFCA can fix — it's the absence of a national patient identifier in US healthcare. TEFCA codifies XCPD (Cross-Community Patient Discovery) but doesn't change the underlying matching problem.

Consent granularity

For IAS, the IAS Provider Requirements SOP v2.1 addresses individual transparency into consent choices. For non-IAS flows (Treatment, Health Care Operations, Public Health, Government Benefits Determination), consent is largely handled out-of-band by the Responding QHIN — it may filter records (behavioral health, substance use, sensitive segments) without surfacing what was filtered. Granular, segment-level consent is implemented inconsistently across QHINs.

Adoption unevenness

21,086 organizations live on TEFCA is a fraction of the ~6,000 hospitals and ~200K clinician practices in the US. Specialty coverage is uneven; behavioral health and LTPAC (long-term and post-acute care) lag the acute and ambulatory side. The "TEFCA reaches X%" framing depends on what you count.

C-CDA → FHIR transition burden

The dual-pipeline reality from Phase 3 isn't going away in 2026 or 2027. Implementers building today design for both XCPD/XCA/C-CDA AND Facilitated FHIR — the migration tax falls on the implementer, not the framework.

Regulatory turbulence (2025–2026)

The legal ground TEFCA stands on is shifting while implementations are maturing. Three policy stories worth watching:

  • TEFCA Manner Exception removal proposal (Dec 2025). The ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Proposed Rule proposed to remove Subpart D of 45 CFR part 171, which includes the TEFCA Manner Exception (§ 171.403). Comment period closed February 27, 2026. Status: proposed, not yet final. If finalized, the regulatory incentive to route requests through TEFCA changes.
  • HTI-4 Final Rule (Aug 2025). Published as part of the FY2026 CMS Hospital IPPS Final Rule, effective October 1, 2025. Covers electronic prior auth, e-prescribing, and real-time prescription benefit criteria.
  • ONC reinstatement (March 31, 2026). HHS reinstated ONC and dropped the "ASTP/ONC" dual title that had been in place since July 2024. The National Coordinator role was un-merged from the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy. Many 2024–2025 documents still use the ASTP/ONC title — citing them as ONC now is correct but historically annotated.

Sources: Federal Register, ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Proposed Rule (Dec 2025); HHS reinstates ONC, ends ASTP dual title.

TEFCA's forward direction · CMS Health Tech Ecosystem

Two parallel tracks shaping what comes next. They are not the same thing.

TEFCA's path

Facilitated FHIR + Government Benefits Determination

Facilitated FHIR SOP v2.0 rollout (draft March 2026; UDAP-based registration permitted through 2027) is the FHIR migration trajectory. Government Benefits Determination — the newest of the six Exchange Purposes — is the SSA disability-determination use case (SSDI Title II, SSI Title XVI), expected to grow through 2026–2027 as more QHINs implement the GBD SOP.

Parallel effort

CMS Health Tech Ecosystem

A 2025 administration initiative: a voluntary information-sharing blueprint with broad healthcare and tech-company participation (early Humana + Epic data-sharing features launched in 2025). Its interaction with TEFCA is still being determined — voluntary CMS-blessed pathways may compete with or complement the more mandate-driven TEFCA path. Worth tracking; not yet a substitute. Context, not endorsement.
